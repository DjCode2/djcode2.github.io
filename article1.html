Accessing a Service Using Kerberos Authentication in 3 Steps :

Several different entities are involved. First, the Client — you. You want to access a Service. To do that, you need a Service Ticket.

This Service Ticket is delivered by the TGS (Ticket Granting Service).
Before giving it to you, the TGS needs to know: Who are you? Are you allowed to access this service?

To prove your identity, you present a TGT (Ticket‑Granting Ticket). The TGT is your proof of authentication,
issued earlier by the Authentication Service (AS). With it, the TGS can verify who you are and decide whether to issue the Service Ticket.

1. AS‑REQ / AS‑REP — Getting the TGT
The client sends an AS‑REQ to the Authentication Service to prove its identity. The AS replies with an AS‑REP containing the TGT (Ticket‑Granting Ticket), which will be used for all later requests.

2. TGS‑REQ / TGS‑REP — Getting the Service Ticket
The client sends a TGS‑REQ to the Ticket Granting Service, presenting the TGT and asking for access to a specific service. The TGS replies with a TGS‑REP containing the Service Ticket.

3. AP‑REQ / AP‑REP — Accessing the Service
The client sends an AP‑REQ to the target service, presenting the Service Ticket. The service validates it and responds with an AP‑REP, granting access.



Security risks across the Kerberos flow

Even if Kerberos never sends passwords over the network, each step introduces specific risks:

AS‑REQ / AS‑REP — vulnerable to AS‑REP Roasting and offline password cracking if pre‑authentication is disabled. A stolen TGT can also be reused (Pass‑the‑Ticket).

TGS‑REQ / TGS‑REP — vulnerable to Kerberoasting: the Service Ticket can be cracked offline if the service account uses a weak password. Misconfigured delegation can also be abused.

AP‑REQ / AP‑REP — vulnerable to Pass‑the‑Ticket and replay attacks. If an attacker knows a service key, they can forge a Silver Ticket and bypass the KDC entirely.
